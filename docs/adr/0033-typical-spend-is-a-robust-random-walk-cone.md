# Typical spend is a one-sided, robust, random-walk cone

Picks up the estimator tuning that [ADR-0028](0028-outlook-anchors-on-the-current-month.md) explicitly deferred ("the trailing 3-month min/median/max is crude... left for later") and sharpens the **Typical spend** definition in [ADR-0027](0027-forward-looking-finance-is-computed-projection.md). The substance of both — computed-never-stored, template-driven, **Occurrence matching**, the current-month anchor — is unchanged. This ADR changes only *what flows into the everyday-spend estimate* and *how its uncertainty grows over the horizon*, because the shipped model produced cones so wide they were useless: a real instance with a stable ~€3k checking balance fanned to roughly −€10k..+€100k over eight months.

Three compounding faults caused that. **(1)** The estimate swept in *every* P&L-touching line, so a single non-recurring **Income** event — a bonus, a tax refund, sale proceeds — landed in the trailing window as a large positive month, the mirror of the "systematically too high" failure ADR-0027 set out to prevent. **(2)** The band edges were the literal *min and max* of only **three** months, so the two most extreme months defined the entire cone with no smoothing. **(3)** The engine added that whole min/max spread onto the running balance **every month** (`balanceLow += band.Low` per iteration), i.e. it assumed the single worst month recurs every month forever *and*, in parallel, the single best month does too — a linear `n × (max − min)` fan. Faults (1) and (3) multiply: a windfall leaks in, becomes the high edge, then replays every month to six figures.

We decide three things. **Typical spend is one-sided everyday *spend*:** it counts only **Expense**-leg movement (refunds credited back to an Expense account legitimately net it upward; non-recurring **Income** is excluded entirely, as are template-matched occurrences and self-transfers, unchanged). **The estimator is robust over a longer window:** the center is the **median** monthly everyday spend over a trailing **six** months (a lone odd month is one-sixth, not one-third, of the sample), and its dispersion is a *robust* spread (median absolute deviation), which a single outlier barely moves — so the original one-off-purchase complaint that motivated this work dies at the source. **Uncertainty grows as a random walk:** each future month is a fresh draw around the median, so the cone half-width at month *n* is `±1 robust-sigma × √n`, a "typical range" rather than a worst-case fan. For users with fewer than six months of history the window degrades gracefully to what exists and the cone is honestly wider.

Rejected / deferred: **keeping net (two-sided) flow** (it is what made the high edge explode; income is either a template or a windfall, never everyday spend). **Keeping the min/median/max band** (interpretable, but min/max of three points is just the two outliers, and there is nothing to fix about *how* it propagates without also fixing *what* it measures). **A linear cone** (`± spread × n`) (still over-wide; months partly cancel, so √n is the honest growth). **A 2-sigma / "95%" band** (defensible, but reads as alarmingly wide for a personal-finance glance; ±1-sigma "typical range" is the better default and the multiplier is a cheap later knob). **A twelve-month window** (more stable spread, but bakes in stale lifestyle and catches more one-offs; start at six). **Mean instead of median** (a mean is exactly what a one-off purchase drags off course — the whole point is to resist that). No ledger columns and no stored projection are introduced; like every **Projection** input this stays a pure function of definitions and current balances.
